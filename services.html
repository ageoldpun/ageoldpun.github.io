Written goals will be provided to guide the individualized therapy based off of the evaluation results, or if your child has had a recent evaluation and current IEP goals, those can be used. Sessions can be at the clinic or at the home of the child.

What is an SLP and what does CCC mean?

SLP stands for speech-language pathologist which is a person who has received a master’s degree in Speech-Language Pathology from an accredited university. The CCC stands for Certificate of Clinical Competence which is awarded by ASHA (American Speech—Language- Hearing Association) and indicates that the SLP has completed a Clinical Fellowship Year after graduation, continues to complete 30 education clock hours every 3 years and is in good standing with ASHA.

What is speech/language therapy?

Speech and language therapy is individualized instruction provided by an SLP to improve speech or language skills in a person who has been diagnosed with a delay or a disorder. Speech therapy focuses on the production of speech sounds and overall intelligibility of speech. Language therapy covers expressive and receptive skills. Expressive language skills include using appropriate vocabulary, grammar and sentence structure to effectively communicate. Expressive language skills also target conversational skills such as turn-taking, topic-maintenance and knowing how to convey a message depending on your audience. Receptive language skills include understanding vocabulary, grammar and sentence structure, as well as interpreting non-verbal communication cues.

What if my child already receives speech/language services through his/her school?

Wonderful! Speech and Language Connections is available to help provide additional support in the hopes of remediating their delay or disorder more quickly. School based services focus on educational impact which may limit the areas that the school SLP can target. Private therapy has the flexibility of treating a wider range of delays and is able to consider the impact of the delay/disorder not only on education, but on daily functioning in the home, in their social group and in the community. Also, if your student has a current IEP and evaluation, an evaluation may not be needed through Speech and Language Connections as the information from the educational assessment may be used to determine goals for your child.

Would my child qualify for speech/language therapy in the private setting if they didn’t qualify in his/her school?

Possibly. The school system is bound by eligibility criteria determined by their district or state that is more limited than a private practice setting. The school-based SLP must determine that there is a significant educational impact that is the result of a speech/language delay. So, a student may have a lisp or a mild stutter, but if the child is academically successful, then the student would not qualify because there would be no significant educational impact. The private setting is not limited by that criteria and can provide therapy for all delays and disorders.

Speech

Area of Service Delay or Disorder Might Sound Like…
Articulation Disorder Your child’s speech is difficult to understand because he mispronounces sounds in words. It could be a deletion, substitutions, or distortion of a sound(s). For example, he might say “toof” for “tooth” or “wain” for “rain”.
Phonological Delay Your child has difficulty with sound patterns and is using a non-age appropriate rule to simply speech. For example, all sounds that are made in the throat (g and k) might be made in the front of the mouth (d and t). So, “cat” becomes “tat” and “cake” becomes “take”.
Apraxia of Speech Your child has difficulty saying sounds, syllables and words due to a motor planning issue, not a developmental delay or impairment with the muscles. Sound errors are inconsistent.
Stuttering Your child has speech that is impacted by repetitions of sounds or words; blocks of silence while trying to produce speech and/or atypical physical movements that accompany a stuttered moment.

Language

Area of Service Delay or Disorder Might Sound Like…
Receptive Language Your child has difficulty understanding age-appropriate vocabulary, grammar, question words or directions.
Expressive Language Your child has difficulty producing language, such as, labeling objects/ pictures, composing a complete sentence, using appropriate grammar or forming questions.
Augmentative/Alternative Communication Your child is non-verbal or has limited verbal abilities and needs an alternative form of communicating. This could be with pictures, sign-language, writing or some type of technology.

Social Language

Area of Service Delay or Disorder Might Sound Like…
Use of Language Your child has difficulty using language to socialize, request, inform, reject and question.
Adaptation Your child has difficulty looking at nonverbal cues to understand how he should be talking to a certain listener or in a certain situation. He might use a loud voice in a quiet setting or use inappropriate language with a teacher.
Conversational Rules Your child has difficulty with using eye contact during conversation, turn-taking or can’t stay on topic. He might have difficulty with telling a story in an organized manner or interpreting non-verbal cues when his listener is confused.
